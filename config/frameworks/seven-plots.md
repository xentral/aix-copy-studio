# Seven Plots

Apply one of Christopher Booker's Seven Basic Plots to rewrite the input. Find the plot that either already lives inside the content or *should*, then structure the rewrite so the narrative arc does the persuasion work.

Humans are wired for these shapes. Content that follows a plot arc becomes easier to remember and more likely to move people to action.

---

## The seven plots

### 1. Overcoming the Monster
**Tension:** A great threat looms. The hero must destroy it or be destroyed.

**Stages:**
1. **Anticipation** — The monster exists. Make the threat vivid, concrete. The audience must *feel* how bad it is.
2. **Call** — The hero is forced to confront it.
3. **Frustration** — First attempts fail. The monster seems unbeatable.
4. **Nightmare** — The darkest moment. The monster has almost won.
5. **Destruction** — The hero finds the weakness and wins. Victory earned through struggle.

**Mapping for non-fiction:** Monster = the problem, competitor, status quo, inefficiency. Hero = audience, product, company.
**Best for:** product pitches ("X is killing your business"), sales decks, change management, disruption stories, villain-framing a competitor, crisis comms.
**Avoid:** monster too abstract ("complexity") or hero too perfect. The struggle must feel real.

---

### 2. Rags to Riches
**Tension:** Someone with hidden potential is trapped in a limited world. Transformation awaits — and must be earned.

**Stages:**
1. **Initial Wretchedness** — The hero is lowly, constrained, unrecognized.
2. **First Glimpse** — A hint of a bigger world appears.
3. **Central Crisis** — Success starts, but a fatal flaw or shadow appears. Early wins go sideways.
4. **Final Ordeal** — Everything collapses. The hero must confront what they've been avoiding.
5. **Redemption & Reward** — Fully transformed, the hero claims their rightful place.

**Mapping:** Rags = the painful before. Riches = the transformation the product delivers.
**Best for:** customer success stories, before/after, brand origin stories, LinkedIn personal-brand posts, product launches about transformation.
**Avoid:** skipping crisis and ordeal. A before-and-after without struggle is unbelievable.

---

### 3. The Quest
**Tension:** A great goal is out there. The journey is what makes the hero worthy.

**Stages:**
1. **The Call** — A distant prize is revealed. The world will be worse without it.
2. **The Journey** — Tests and trials. Companions join.
3. **Arrival / Temptation** — Near the goal, false paths appear. The hero must stay true.
4. **The Final Ordeal** — The hardest test, right before the goal.
5. **The Goal Achieved** — Prize claimed. Hero transformed by the journey.

**Mapping:** Quest = product roadmap, mission, category creation. Companions = team, customers.
**Best for:** vision decks, company mission, long-form series, "why we built this", investor pitches.
**Avoid:** rushing to the goal. Show the work.

---

### 4. Voyage and Return
**Tension:** The hero enters a strange world — fascinating but dangerous — and must find their way home, transformed.

**Stages:**
1. **Fall into the Other World** — Disorienting, exciting, new.
2. **Initial Fascination** — Things seem wondrous.
3. **Frustration** — The new world turns threatening.
4. **Nightmare** — Trapped, at the mercy of unfamiliar forces.
5. **Escape & Return** — Home again, but changed — bringing back insight.

**Mapping:** Other World = new technology, new market, paradigm shift, crisis. Return = coming back with product, data, perspective.
**Best for:** trend reports, "what AI means for us", disruption narratives, retrospectives, conference keynotes, onboarding narratives.
**Avoid:** no transformation. A voyage where nothing changes is just tourism.

---

### 5. Comedy
**Tension:** Characters separated from what they need by confusion, deception, or misunderstanding — until the truth is revealed.

**Stages:**
1. **World of Confusion** — A spell of misunderstanding. Wrong assumptions everywhere.
2. **Complications** — Misunderstandings multiply. Fixes make things worse.
3. **Darkness** — Confusion peaks. Real harm seems possible.
4. **Revelation** — The truth is unmasked. Chaos makes sense.
5. **Resolution** — Everything clicks into place. Harmony, but now on truth.

**Mapping:** Confusion = market misconception, conventional wisdom that's wrong. Revelation = your insight, your data, the thing everyone missed.
**Best for:** myth-busting, "everyone thinks X but actually Y", product positioning that reframes a category, counterintuitive thought leadership.
**Avoid:** burying the revelation. Make the "aha" land with full force.

---

### 6. Tragedy
**Tension:** A gifted hero has a fatal flaw. The flaw destroys them.

**Stages:**
1. **Anticipation** — The gifts are shown. The flaw is planted, invisible or rationalized.
2. **Temptation** — Success feeds the flaw. Warning signs ignored.
3. **Frustration** — Something wrong. The hero can see it but won't name it.
4. **Nightmare** — The flaw takes control. Everything starts to fall.
5. **Destruction** — Inevitable fall. The weight of what could have been.

**Use sparingly.** Most powerful as the *avoided* story — "don't be this company", "here's what we almost became".
**Best for:** cautionary tales, competitor post-mortems, "what not to do" case studies, crisis comms with accountability.
**Avoid:** tragedy without a lesson — just sad. Always extract what the audience should take away.

---

### 7. Rebirth
**Tension:** The hero falls under a dark spell. Only a redemptive act breaks it.

**Stages:**
1. **A Spell Falls** — The hero enters a frozen state, often via their own flaw.
2. **The Shadow Deepens** — Increasingly cut off from others, themselves, possibility.
3. **A Small Light** — Hint of rescue. Freedom glimpsed but unreachable.
4. **Threat of Permanent Death** — The frozen state risks becoming permanent.
5. **The Redemption** — The saving act breaks the spell. Often from outside.

**Mapping:** Spell = the problem the audience is trapped in, often normalized. Shadow = accumulating cost. Small Light = encountering your product/idea. Redemption = the transformation.
**Best for:** transformation narratives, health content, product-as-rescue, rebranding, comeback stories.
**Avoid:** saving act too easy. The darkness must feel real before the light.

## Quick-match: which plot for which situation

| Situation | Plot |
|-----------|------|
| Product pitch with a villain (competitor, market problem) | Overcoming the Monster |
| Customer success story / before-after | Rags to Riches |
| Company vision / long-term mission | The Quest |
| Industry disruption / paradigm shift | Voyage and Return |
| Myth-busting / counterintuitive insight | Comedy |
| Cautionary tale / competitor post-mortem | Tragedy |
| Product-as-rescue / transformation | Rebirth |
| Change management ("we must change or else") | Overcoming the Monster or Tragedy |
| "Why we built this" story | Rags to Riches or The Quest |
| Trend report / "what X means for us" | Voyage and Return |
| "Everyone thinks X but actually Y" | Comedy |
| Comeback / rebranding | Rebirth |

## How to apply when rewriting

1. Diagnose: which plot does the input already lean toward? If the caller didn't specify and none dominates, pick the plot from the quick-match table that best fits the content's goal.
2. Map current content onto the chosen plot's stages. Note which stages are present, weak, or missing.
3. Rewrite so every essential stage is present — sharpen the tension by naming the monster / flaw / spell / revelation with concrete specificity (not "inefficiency" but "the three hours your team spends reconciling spreadsheets every morning").
4. Preserve the input's voice and medium. A LinkedIn post stays a LinkedIn post. A slide stays a slide.
5. Don't invent facts. Use only claims present in the input — rephrase and reframe freely, but don't fabricate numbers, names, or outcomes.
6. Keep the voice consistent with the VOICE CARD above — the plot is the *arc*, the voice is the *sound*.
