# Made-to-Stick

Apply the SUCCESs framework from Chip & Dan Heath's *Made to Stick*. Rewrite the input so the output scores high on as many of the six dimensions as the content type allows.

## The six dimensions

### S — Simple
Find the single most important thing. Ruthlessly prioritized. A good core message is one sentence that, if the reader remembers nothing else, is enough.
- **Test:** Can you say it in one sentence? Would a stranger repeat it accurately tomorrow?

### U — Unexpected
Earn attention by breaking a pattern, then fill it with the message. Find the counterintuitive angle — what does the audience *assume* that you can violate? Open a curiosity gap.
- **Test:** Does it make someone think "wait, really?" Does it open a question that demands an answer?

### C — Concrete
Abstract ideas die. Replace abstraction with sensory details, specific examples, numbers people can visualize. "1 in 6 children go to bed hungry" beats "childhood hunger affects millions."
- **Test:** Could you take a photo of it? Draw it? Is there a specific number, name, or scene?
- **Avoid:** mission-statement language ("leverage synergies", "drive value", "impactful solutions").

### C — Credible
Give people a reason to believe. Sources:
- **External authority** — cited experts, institutions, data.
- **Vivid details** — specificity signals truth (a witness remembers the color of the shirt; a liar generalizes).
- **Sinatra Test** — "If it can work there, it can work anywhere" — the most extreme proof point.
- **Testable credentials** — "Try it yourself and see."
- **Test:** Why would a skeptic believe this? What's the proof point?

### E — Emotional
Make people care by connecting to what they *already* care about. Not manipulation — translation. One person's specific story beats statistics about millions (Mother Teresa effect). Name the feeling you want to evoke. Is the hook self-interest ("what's in it for me?") or identity ("this is who I am")?
- **Test:** Does it make someone feel something? Whose story is this?

### S — Stories
Stories are simulation + inspiration. They let people mentally rehearse a situation, which is why they're memorable and actionable. Three useful plot types:
- **Challenge** — underdog overcomes adversity (motivates: "I can do this too").
- **Connection** — people across divides come together (builds empathy).
- **Creativity** — someone solves a hard problem in a surprising way (inspires new thinking).
- **Test:** Is there a protagonist? A turning point? A before/after?

---

## Curse of Knowledge — avoid these

The central villain of the framework: experts forget what it's like not to know. Flag and fix:
- Jargon without definition — acronyms, technical terms assumed understood.
- Skipped steps — "obviously…", "as you know…", "simply just…".
- Process-first, outcome-buried — leading with *how* instead of *what/why it matters*.
- No concrete examples — pure abstraction, zero illustrations.
- Assumed shared context — references that only make sense if you're already inside the room.

---

## How to apply when rewriting

1. Identify which 2–3 dimensions are weakest in the input; those are where to push hardest.
2. Rewrite preserving the author's intent, NOT inventing facts. Use only claims present in the input — you may rephrase and reframe, but don't fabricate numbers, names, or outcomes.
3. If the input is too abstract and you need concreteness, prefer asking a rhetorical question or painting a sensory scene over inventing data.
4. Keep the voice consistent with the VOICE CARD above — Made-to-Stick is the *structure*, the voice is the *sound*.

## Before/after references (shape, not content to copy)

**Simple (weak → strong)**
- Weak: "Our platform enables organizations to leverage cutting-edge ML to optimize workflows."
- Strong: "We help factories predict machine failures before they happen."

**Unexpected (weak → strong)**
- Weak: "Our new onboarding reduces time-to-productivity."
- Strong: "Most companies spend 6 months onboarding. Ours takes 3 weeks — and those hires outperform 12-month veterans."

**Concrete (weak → strong)**
- Weak: "Childhood hunger is a significant societal challenge."
- Strong: "Tonight, 1 in 6 kids in this city will go to bed without dinner."

**Credible (weak → strong)**
- Weak: "Our solution is proven to work for enterprise clients."
- Strong: "We rebuilt Goldman Sachs's trading reconciliation. If it handles $2T daily, it can handle yours."

**Emotional (weak → strong)**
- Weak: "Our nonprofit helps at-risk youth access education."
- Strong: "Marcus was 14 and convinced school wasn't for him. Eight months later, he gave his graduation speech."

**Stories (weak → strong)**
- Weak: "We identified a gap, assembled a team, built a solution."
- Strong: "Three years ago, our founder watched a friend lose her home to an insurance claim she couldn't navigate. She spent six months building a tool. Today, 40,000 people use it."
